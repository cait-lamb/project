What is Color Theory?

Color theory is the axis on which the world of design spins. It influences perception, helps steer the audience's attention in the direction the designer wishes, and can draw emotions with the slightest shift. This powerful design tool, when properly used, can enhance visual compoenents and communicate without words. This is further explored on the Color Theory Article page where the color palettes on a handful of websites is compared.

Below is a list of color theory terms that are useful to know in design but also in everyday life. Learning and understanding these principles allows for more awareness when viewing art in any form of media.

  • Color Wheel: A fundamental tool in color theory that illustrates the relationsips between colors. It also aids designers in selecting complementary colors, triadic, and so on with ease.
  • Primary Colors: Pure colors that cannot be created by mixing other colors; red, blue, and yellow.
  • Secondary Colors: Colors formed by mixing two primary colors; green, violet, and orange.
  • Tertiary Colors: Colors formed by mixing a primary and secondary color together; blue-violet, blue-green, yellow-green, yellow-orange, red-orange, and red-violet.
  • Saturation: Refers to the intensity and purity of a color. The higher the saturation, the more vivid the colors and lower saturation results in more muted tones.
  • Vibrancy: The brightness, intensity, and energy of a hue. Generally speaking, vibrant colors build excitement and feel bold from the brightness.
  • Contrasting: Combining different hues to highlight their differences. The higher the contrast, the sharper the difference whereas low contrast will present vague boundaries.
  • Hues: The purest form of a color. The twelve primary, secondary, and tertiary colors are considered the purest and most vivid.
  • Shades: Adds black to a hue.
  • Tints: Adds white to a hue.
  • Tones: Adds gray to a hue.
  • Neutral Colors: When next to a pure color, neutral colors can look like they have no color but they usually have subtle undertones.
  • Warm Colors: Colors that bring energy and provide a sense of warmth. Warm colors are toward the red side of the color wheel.
  • Cool Colors: Colors that give feelings of calm and present a cooler temperature. Cool colors are on the blue side of the color wheel.
  • Monochromatic: A color scheme containing various shades, tones, and tints of a single hue. Patterns and textures often stand out more in a monochromatic scheme.
  • Complementary Colors: These colors are on opposite sides of the color wheel and are usually used for a strong visual contrast, drawing attention, or adding emphasis.
  • Triadic Colors: Groups of three colors that are evenly spaced on the color wheel. The colors are balanced while still providing good contrast. The most common approach is to choose a dominant color and use the other two as accents.
  • Split Colors: Used to avoid the potential harsh contrast with direct complementary colors. Split colors are variations of color families by using analagous hues of complementary colors.
  • Analogous Colors: Groups of colors next to each other on the color wheel.
  • Color Palette: An effective color palette will include labeled color swatches (including RGB, CMYK, and hexadecimal), hierarchy of usage, special instructions. Color palettes will often contain a theme, discussed further on the Color Themes page.
